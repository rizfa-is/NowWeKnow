## A 19th-century puzzle

People had wondered about the colour of the sky for centuries. It was
**Lord Rayleigh** in 1871 who put the maths on it, showing that small
particles scatter light in proportion to **1/λ⁴** — a strong preference for
short wavelengths.

::callout
Rayleigh's law is what's behind **both** the blue daytime sky and the
warm tones at sunrise and sunset. It's the same physics, viewed from
different geometries.
::

## A quick recap

- **White sunlight** is a mix of every visible wavelength.
- **Air molecules** are far smaller than the wavelengths of visible light.
- That size mismatch makes molecules **scatter blue and violet** much more
  than red.
- Our eyes are more sensitive to blue than violet, and the upper atmosphere
  absorbs some violet. So the sky reads as blue.

## What about sunsets?

When the Sun is low, its light slices through a much thicker slab of
atmosphere. By the time it reaches you, the blue has been scattered out
of the direct beam — leaving the warm orange and red tones of the Sun
itself, sitting on a sky still painted blue overhead.

## Try this

Hold up a glass of water with a drop of milk in it and shine a torch
through. The water lights up faintly blue from the side, and the torch
beam looks orange from the front. That's Rayleigh scattering on your
kitchen counter.
